Core Web3 Concepts

Side-by-side comparisons to help you understand the building blocks of decentralized technology — explained simply, not like a textbook.

🌐

Web2 vs Web3

Web2 (Centralized)

  • Companies own and control your data on their servers
  • You need permission from platforms to build or participate
  • Accounts can be banned or data deleted at any time
  • Revenue flows to platform owners, not creators

Web3 (Decentralized)

  • You own your data, assets, and digital identity via wallets
  • Anyone can build on open protocols without asking
  • No single entity can shut you down or erase your history
  • Value is shared between users, developers, and token holders
₿

Ethereum vs Bitcoin

Bitcoin

  • Created in 2009 as digital gold — a store of value
  • Designed primarily for peer-to-peer payments
  • Limited scripting — not built for complex applications
  • ~10 minute block times, capped at ~7 transactions per second

Ethereum

  • Launched in 2015 as a programmable blockchain platform
  • Supports smart contracts — self-executing code on-chain
  • Powers DeFi, NFTs, DAOs, and thousands of dApps
  • Transitioned to Proof of Stake, enabling faster finality
🔑

Public Key vs Private Key

Private Key

  • A secret 256-bit number — your password to the blockchain
  • Never share it with anyone; losing it means losing access forever
  • Used to sign transactions and prove you own an account
  • Think of it like the key to a safe only you can open

Public Key / Address

  • Derived from your private key through one-way cryptography
  • Safe to share — it's your wallet address for receiving funds
  • Others can verify your signatures without seeing your private key
  • Like a mailbox address: anyone can send mail, only you can open it
🗄️

Blockchain vs Traditional Databases

Traditional Database

  • Controlled by a single organization (Google, a bank, etc.)
  • Admins can edit, delete, or roll back records at will
  • Fast and efficient for internal business operations
  • Users must trust the company to handle data honestly

Blockchain

  • Data is replicated across thousands of independent nodes
  • Once confirmed, records are extremely difficult to alter
  • Slower and more expensive, but trustless and transparent
  • Anyone can verify the data — no need to trust a central authority
